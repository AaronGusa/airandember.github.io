NEO-
BRUTALISM

Loud. Honest. On purpose.

Neo-brutalism is the friendly, design-school cousin of raw web brutalism. Where classic brutalism (see our brutalist example page) embraces unstyled 90s HTML as a protest against slick sameness, neo-brutalism keeps the bluntness but adds deliberate craft. It bubbled up through the Figma and Gumroad era of the early 2020s, when product brands wanted to look hand-made and confident instead of corporate and interchangeable.

The signature is a three-part recipe you can spot from across the room: flat, saturated color fills with zero gradients; thick solid black borders (3–4px, never hairline); and hard offset shadows with no blur at all — a solid black slab sitting behind every card, as if the page were cut from paper and glued together. Type is oversized and unapologetic, buttons physically move toward their shadow when pressed, and sticker-style badges shout in all caps.

Small businesses love it because it is memorable, honest, and cheap to execute — no photography budget, no illustration pipeline, just color, borders, and conviction. The pitfall: overdone, it tips into gimmick. If every element screams, nothing does — the best neo-brutalist sites pair the loud frames with disciplined spacing and very plain body text, exactly like the card you are reading now.

Demo 1

The Shadow Forge

The hard, unblurred shadow IS the signature. Drag the sliders: the shadow is just box-shadow: Xpx Ypx 0 0 black — that third value (blur) stays locked at zero. Add even a little blur and the card turns into ordinary “material design” floating paper; keep it sharp and the card looks die-cut and glued on. Border thickness works the same way: 1px reads as polite stationery, 4px reads as a statement.

Demo 2

Clash, but curated

Neo-brutalist palettes look random. They are not. Each one is a small set of loud colors balanced for contrast against black borders and a calm base. Hit the button to cycle through five curated palettes — every accent block, sticker, and button on this page re-inks itself through CSS custom properties. Notice that the colors always clash on purpose: high saturation, mismatched hues, but never more than three or four accents at once.

Current palette: Ember & Pine — the Air&Ember house colors, turned up loud.

Demo 3

What if we “made it professional”?

Every small business hears it eventually: “can we tone it down?” Flip the switch and this entire page converts to generic corporate flat design — thin grey borders, soft blurred shadows, muted colors, smaller type. Nothing breaks. It is perfectly usable. It is also instantly forgettable: the page now looks like ten thousand other pages. Flip it back and notice how much personality the black borders and hard shadows were carrying.

Flat fills

One saturated color per block. No gradients, no textures, no apologies.

Thick strokes

3–4px solid black outlines make every element feel drawn by hand with a marker.

Pressed hovers
