Soft. Tactile. Sculpted from the page.

Neumorphism (short for “new skeuomorphism”) makes interface elements look as if they were pressed out of — or into — the background itself. Instead of borders or flat color blocks, it relies on one soft background tone, gentle gradients, and a pair of shadows: a light one on the side facing the light source and a dark one opposite it. The result feels physical, calm, and quietly premium.

Honest caveat: because everything is the same color and edges are defined only by soft shadows, neumorphism scores poorly on contrast and accessibility. Users with low vision can struggle to find buttons at all. Most real products use it sparingly — as an accent, not a whole system.

Demo 1 — It’s all just lighting

Every neumorphic shadow on this page is computed from one shared “light direction.” Pick a corner below and watch the entire page relight itself — the header, cards, buttons, everything. That consistency is the whole trick: neumorphism is simulated lighting, and it only works if every element agrees on where the light is coming from.

Demo 2 — The three classic states

Neumorphic elements live in one of three states. Raised casts outward shadows, as if extruded from the surface. Flat sits flush — only a subtle edge separates it. Pressed flips the shadows inward (inset), as if pushed into the surface. Click each chip; the “Press Me” buttons across this page use the raised→pressed transition on click.

Neumorphism Basics

Soft backgrounds, gentle dual shadows, and subtle gradients create tactile, almost tangible interfaces — merging digital design with a physically inspired, organic aesthetic.

Clean, Modern Layout

Neumorphic layouts lean on simplicity: ample whitespace, refined typography, and balanced structure that lets the softly raised elements do the talking.

Responsiveness

Because depth comes from shadows rather than fixed imagery, neumorphic interfaces adapt gracefully across devices while keeping their tactile feel intact.

The Glass Cousin

This frosted panel is glassmorphism — neumorphism’s sibling trend. Where neumorphism fakes depth with shadows on an opaque surface, glassmorphism fakes it with translucency and background blur. Same goal, opposite material.
