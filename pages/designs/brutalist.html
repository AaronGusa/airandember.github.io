Brutalist web design is a bold, unconventional approach that deliberately rejects modern design trends like smooth interfaces and polished minimalism. Inspired by the architectural Brutalism movement of the mid-20th century — which favored raw, unadorned concrete — it embraces a raw, utilitarian look: stark layouts, unconventional typography, and a cheerful disregard for traditional usability norms.

At its core, Brutalism is functional and straightforward, presenting content in a way that feels unfiltered and unapologetic. Sites in this style favor minimal CSS, monochrome palettes plus one screaming accent, oversized text, visible underlines, and bare HTML elements like the humble <hr>. It thrives on contrast: clashing colors, harsh lines, and blocky elements that refuse the harmony of typical layouts.

While often perceived as polarizing, Brutalism has a loyal following in creative industries — art, fashion, independent media — where its rawness reads as a statement of authenticity in a landscape of homogenized, sanitized templates.

The catch: rejecting usability on purpose can alienate users who don't speak the visual language. The designer's challenge is balancing the edge with function, so the site stays accessible and navigable even while it's yelling at you.

Ultimately, Brutalist web design is a provocation — it turns simplicity and harshness into an artistic statement, and dares you to call it a bug.
