Glassmorphism

Frosted panes floating over light.

Glassmorphism styles interface surfaces as panes of frosted glass: semi-transparent fills, a heavy background blur, a hair-thin light border, and a soft shadow that lifts each pane off the scenery behind it. The look traces back to the “vibrancy” materials of iOS 7 (2013), matured into a full design language with macOS Big Sur (2020), and runs in parallel on Windows as the Acrylic material in Fluent Design. The defining trait is that the surface is never a flat color — it is a live sample of whatever sits behind it, blurred until it reads as texture instead of content.

Its strengths are depth and elegance: layered panes create an instant sense of hierarchy (closer panes are sharper and brighter), and a well-tuned blur makes even a riotous background feel calm. The honest caveats: legibility depends entirely on what wanders behind the glass, so text contrast must be checked against the worst case, not the prettiest screenshot; and the CSS that powers it, backdrop-filter, while now supported in all modern browsers, still deserves an @supports fallback — this page swaps to a more opaque fill wherever blur is unavailable, and you should too.

Use glassmorphism when you have rich imagery or color to show through — hero overlays, media players, dashboards over data visualizations. Skip it for dense text-heavy products, where the shifting background becomes noise.

Demo 1 — The Glass Lab

The entire frosted-glass recipe is three ingredients. Drag each slider and watch the sample pane: blur radius is the frost (at 0 the pane is clear plastic, not glass), fill opacity is the milkiness of the white tint (too high and it becomes a plain white card), and border opacity is the lit edge that sells the pane as a physical sheet catching light. Each control writes a CSS custom property — the sample's style is just backdrop-filter: blur(var(--lab-blur)) plus a translucent fill and border.

Frosted sample

Aurora light passes through this pane. The blur turns the chaos behind it into a soft glow.

Demo 2 — Glass needs scenery

A frosted pane is only as interesting as what it frosts. Switch the page background and watch every card on this page: over the aurora the glass glows, over the photo it reads as a window, and over flat grey the blur has nothing to do — the “glass” collapses into an ordinary grey rectangle. If your product's background is a solid color, glassmorphism is the wrong tool.

Currently blurring: layered gradient aurora — the ideal habitat for glass.

Demo 3 — Layered depth

Depth in glassmorphism comes from stacking. The rear pane below blurs the aurora; the front pane blurs the rear pane and the aurora, so it looks unmistakably closer. Notice the front pane also gets a slightly brighter fill and border — nearer glass catches more light. Hover (or tap) either pane to lift it.

Rear pane

Dimmer fill, softer edge. Reads as background chrome.

Front pane

Brighter fill, crisper border, deeper shadow. Reads as the active layer.
