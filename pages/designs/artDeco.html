Ornament With Discipline

Art Deco took its name from the 1925 Exposition Internationale des Arts Décoratifs in Paris, and its spirit from the roaring decade around it — Gatsby’s parties, ocean liners, jazz, and a wild new optimism about machines. Where Art Nouveau had curled like a vine, Deco snapped to attention: sunbursts, chevrons, ziggurats, and fan motifs, all drafted with a ruler and compass. Its cathedral is the Chrysler Building, whose stainless-steel crown of stacked, radiating arches is essentially a repeating-conic-gradient rendered in chrome.

The traits travel to the web intact: strict bilateral symmetry, hard geometry, vertical emphasis, all-caps letterspaced typography, and — crucially — metallics used as the accent palette. Gold, brass, and chrome do the job that a bright brand color does elsewhere. What makes Deco feel expensive rather than busy is restraint of placement: ornament is confined to borders, frames, dividers, and backgrounds, executed in repeating geometric units, while the content itself stays clean. That is why luxury brands, hotels, restaurants, event invitations, and anything wanting “1920s glamour” still reach for it a century later.

The pitfall is letting the gilding swallow the message. Metallic gradient text at paragraph sizes, patterns behind body copy, and ornamental corners on every element turn glamour into noise — and gold-on-black contrast is less forgiving than it looks. Use Deco when the brand genuinely trades on elegance, ceremony, or nostalgia, hold the ornament to the edges of the composition, and let generous black space do half the work. Wind the dial on this page up and down once more: the geometry survives at every level — the ornament is the costume, not the skeleton.

Geometry

Sunbursts, chevrons, and stepped ziggurats — ornament built from ruler-and-compass repetition, never freehand flourish.

Symmetry

Compositions balance on a center axis. The eye is escorted down the middle like a guest down a grand staircase.

Metallics

Gold and chrome are the accent color. Light itself becomes part of the palette — which is why deco loves the dark.
